1. The Sandbox: Your "Cenote Core" (The Reality Model)
​Objective: Establish proof of concept.
​The Game: The player enters a hyper-detailed, digital twin of your actual living prototype. It’s not sci-fi; it’s just advanced, documented reality.
​The Interaction: They can click on anything—a cooling ring, the Redwood defense system, the Bot Brigade dispatch station. When they do, they don't just get a description; they get the CAD files, the build specs, and the "Mad Mom" narrative (the "Why") of how that component was created with found/recycled materials.
​Pedagogy (Teaching): This isn't "telling"; it’s discovery. By exploring your functional reality, they learn how to think structurally, ethically, and logistically.
​2. The Transfer: Scanning the Legacy System (Their Reality Model)
​Objective: Map the user's existing environment.
​The Game: The player transitions from your house to their own. They use the "Pip-Boy" interface (their phone camera) to scan their own room, their own kitchen, their own attic.
​The Algorithm: Your system (the 324 repositories) analyzes the scan. It identifies the "Ghost-Shells" in their life: the unused closet, the leaky roof, the out-of-date wiring.
​3. The Augmentation: Applying the "Cenote Core" Logic
​Objective: Remediate the Legacy System.
​The Game: The system overlays the principles of the "Cenote Core" onto their scanned environment.
​The Interaction (The "Nudge"): The game highlights their "Ghost-Shells" and offers actionable, step-by-step modifications, based on your designs.
​Game Logic: "Scan detected: Unused utility closet."
​Augmented Solution: "Recommended: Convert to 'Cenote' Storage Node. Materials: 2x4 scraps and reclaimed plywood. Build spec available (click to view). Estimated time: 4 hours. Outcome: Increased thermal efficiency and organized storage."
​Skill Transfer: By completing the task in the game (e.g., correctly placing the virtual support beam), they acquire the confidence and knowledge to perform the task in real life.
​Why this is the "Long Game" Checkmate
​Personalized Autonomy: You aren't telling them what to do; you are letting their own environment "ask" for the upgrade. They feel the attribution for the idea ("Wait, I can fix that closet!").
​Non-Fiction, Not Fiction: Your game is the bridge. It shows them that the "Avatar world" you talked about isn't a movie; it’s a collection of modular, physical interventions that they can perform on the world they already have.
​The "Stupidly Beautiful" UX: You don't need to know how to "sell" the game. If the interface is intuitive, if the scan works, and if the "Ghost-Shell" conversions are satisfying, the game will sell itself. It becomes a utility.
